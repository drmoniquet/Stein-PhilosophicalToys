An intertextual reader · Gertrude Stein, Tender Buttons, 1914

“You should always paint knowledge which you have acquired….”i (Stein, “Transatlantic Interview” 31)

“…nothing strange a single hurt color and an arrangement in a system to pointing.” (Stein, Tender Buttons, “A Carafe, that is a Blind Glass”)

Tender Buttons can be understood as a linguistic experiment stretching the limits of language and the poetic genre, a scientific experiment stretching the limits of empirical observation, and even a psychological experiment in rendering in words the stream of consciousness. However, the poem’s importance comes into focus when it is understood as a radical philosophical experiment that offers a sustained critique of Western epistemology, its foundational postulates (such as knowledge, rationality, certainty, truth) as well as its methods (reasoning, measuring, believing). Pass the magnifying glass over Stein’s text to bring forth best guesses at her web of philosophical intertexts.
